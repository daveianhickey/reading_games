# Educational Context: How Children Learn

## The "Sound Button" Method
In UK classrooms, children use "sound buttons" to decode words:
    • Dots: Placed under single letters (e.g., c • a • t •).
    • Dashes: Placed under digraphs or trigraphs to show they belong together (e.g., sh _ i • p •).
    • Blending: The process of saying each sound in order and then "pushing" them together to say the word (e.g., /sh/ - /i/ - /p/ ... ship!).

## Tricky Words (Non-Decodable)
Children are also taught "Tricky Words" (or Common Exception Words) that cannot be fully sounded out using these rules. The AI should use a minimum on one and maximum of three to help sentences flow:
    • the, to, I, no, put, of, is, to, go, into, pull, as, his, he, she buses, e, me, be, push, was, her, my, you, they, all, are, ball, tall, when, what, said, so, have, were, out, like, some, come, there, little, one, do, children, love, oh, their, a people, mr, mrs, your, ask, should, would, could, asked, house, mouse, water, want, very.

## Sentence Guidelines for the AI Agent
When generating content, the AI agent should adhere to these "Level 1" reading constraints:
    1. CVC Focus: Keep most words to Consonant-Vowel-Consonant patterns.
    2. Short Length: Sentences should ideally be 5–8 words long.
    3. Single Focus: Each sentence should highlight one or two specific digraphs/trigraphs.
    4. No Split Digraphs: Avoid words like "cake" or "home" (magic 'e'), as these are usually taught later in Year 1 (Phase 5).
